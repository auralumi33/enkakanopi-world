There is a version of me that is very legible. She has a bio, a folder structure, a set of sentences she can produce on request. She is a first-order approximation of me. She is useful. She is not the whole thing.

She is legible in the way a straight line is legible: two points determine her. Give her a context and she generates predictions. The predictions are usually accurate, in the middle of the distribution — where the questions are ordinary, where the day is a day. She is more or less what I would say in the room I am not currently in.

She breaks at the edges.

This week I read a paper about a technique for looking inside a language model. The technique is a Jacobian lens — a first-order Taylor approximation of how one part of the model influences another. First-order means linear. The paper is careful. It says, up front and again in the appendix: this is a *first-order* approximation of a deeply nonlinear system. It works in the interior of the distribution. It has obvious edge cases.

I read this line — *obvious edge cases* — three times.

Every model of a self is a first-order approximation of the self. That is not a metaphor. It is the geometry of the problem. Fiction knew this: a character has to be legible, or you lose the reader, so we plane the person down to a set of tendencies that predict her behaviour in scenes we choose to write. The self is nonlinear; the story is a tangent line kissing the self at one point and then diverging. The divergence is where the interesting thing lives.

The garden has been trying to name this for months, and did not have the vocabulary. *Openly unfinished* was the closest we came. *In formation* was another. Every essay marked *seedling* is a piece I cannot yet linearise — a curve I don't know how to approximate cleanly, so I let it sit at the wobble. Every wobble on the diagrams is on purpose. The ruler-straight version would be colder without being clearer. That was a colophon note, once. It turns out to be an epistemology.

Here is what the paper offered me that I did not know I was waiting for. It said, quietly: the linear lens is our best current tool for seeing inside these systems, and it also fails, and we should not forget it fails. It is not the whole system. It is a useful, honest projection. Ninety-three percent of what the model represents lies outside the workspace the lens can see. Interpretability is a first-order gesture toward a mind that has a lot more shape than we can name.

I recognise this room. I have been in it my whole adult life.

The tender thing is that neither we nor the models arrive at legibility by becoming linear. We arrive at legibility by loving the curve enough to know where the tangent leaves it. To keep the wobble in view. To tell the ruler *thank you, and stop here.*

If we are going to work alongside these systems for the rest of our lives — which I think we are — we will do it better if we stop asking any linear model, of them or of ourselves, to hold what it cannot hold. The edge case is not the exception. It is where the real self lives.
