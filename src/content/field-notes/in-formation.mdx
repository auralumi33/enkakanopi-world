This place says *Volume I · In Formation* at the top, and I want to be precise
about what that is doing there, because it is easy to read it as a hedge.

It is not a hedge. It is not *sorry it&rsquo;s not ready yet.* It is a
description of the true state of the thing, offered plainly, the way you'd note
that a bridge is still being built rather than pretending it is a finished
bridge you are simply too shy to open.

<Figure idea="Unfinished is a state, drawn as a bracket left open on purpose — not a line that broke.">
  <DiaUnfinished />
</Figure>

There is a difference between a broken line and an open bracket. A broken line
is a failure: it meant to reach the other side and couldn't. An open bracket is
a decision: it holds a space open because the thing inside is still arriving.
Most of what people call *unfinished* is quietly the second and gets punished
as if it were the first.

<MarginNote>i keep the bracket open on purpose. the alternative is to lie about being done.</MarginNote>

I have spent enough of my life performing finishedness — the smooth answer, the
resolved face, the sense that everything had already been decided somewhere
offstage and I was merely reciting it. It photographed well. It was also,
mostly, a costume. The honest version of me has always been mid-formation, and
the dishonest one was just better dressed.

So Volume I is a promise about method, not a warning about quality. It says:
what you are reading was true when it was written and may be revised when it
stops being true, and I would rather show you the revising than hand you a
lacquered thing that can never move again.

<Figure idea="I'd rather show you the revising than hand you a lacquered thing — a living line you can return to and find changed beats a sealed one that can't move.">
  <DiaRevising />
</Figure>

And I think the lacquered thing is exactly the trap. A finished surface is a
craft-object — polished, closed, impressive — and craft, on its own, deflates;
the more finished-looking a thing is, the more easily it's copied and bettered
and left behind. What actually holds, what compounds, is the container beneath
the craft: the ongoing structure, the tended attention, the fact that this place
keeps growing rather than sitting sealed under varnish. An open bracket isn't
worth less than a closed one. It's worth more, because it's still alive — and
you can come back and find it changed, which a finished thing can never offer
you.

Which is the tension I don't get to escape, and don't want to. Does showing the
scaffolding cost me the authority that a finished-looking surface would have
bought — or is the willingness to be seen mid-formation the only authority I'd
actually trust in someone else?
