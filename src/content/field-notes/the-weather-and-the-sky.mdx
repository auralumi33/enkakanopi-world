Here is a small thing that took me an embarrassingly long time to learn, and I
offer it to you in case you're still where I was: a mood is *weather.* It is not
the sky.

For most of my life I read every passing state as a verdict on the whole of me.
A grey afternoon meant I was, fundamentally, a grey person. A storm of doubt
meant the doubt was true and permanent and load-bearing. I let the weather write
directly into the self — no gate, no pause — and so every squall felt like a
final report on who I was, filed and stamped.

<MarginNote>the storm felt like the truth about me. it was just the truth about tuesday.</MarginNote>

But the sky is not the weather that crosses it. The sky is what the weather
happens *in* — the wide steady thing underneath, unharmed by the storm and
un-flattered by the sun, that was there before the front rolled through and will
be there after it clears. This is, if you like, the container beneath the craft
turned inward: the weather is the visible, changeable surface; the sky is the
container it passes through, and the container is the part that holds.

There's a gentler kind of attention that comes with this, once you stop bracing.
You can watch a mood the way you watch real weather through a window — with a
soft fascination, a mild curiosity, *ah, rain again* — instead of climbing
inside it and mistaking it for the room. The mood still comes. You still get
wet. But you are no longer *only* the storm; you're also the sky it's borrowing
for the afternoon.

And still I won't tie the ribbon, because some weather doesn't feel like weather
— some of it feels like a message you'd be a fool to ignore. So the question I
keep, and hand to you at the window: how do you tell the storm that is only
weather from the storm that is trying, at last, to tell you something true — and
what does it cost to guess wrong in either direction?
