木漏れ日. *Canopy.* The light that falls through moving leaves. There is no
single English word for it, and I think that is the point — you cannot hold
it still long enough to name it once and for all.

If you photograph canopy and freeze one frame, you get almost nothing: a few
pale blots on the ground, a smear that could be anything. The beauty was never
in any single instant. It was in the *changing* — the way the pattern breathes
because the canopy moves and the light has to keep finding new gaps.

</Figure>

I used to want a still frame of myself. One good photograph I could hand to
people so they would know, finally and permanently, who they were dealing
with. I wanted to be legible the way a document is legible.

<MarginNote>a document is legible because it stopped moving. i had confused that with being understood.</MarginNote>

But every still frame of me is a lie of the same kind as the frozen canopy —
technically accurate, entirely dead. The version caught mid-sentence, the
version between two cities, the version who has changed her mind since you last
checked: none of them is the real one, and that is exactly why they are all
true. I am only readable in motion. Stop me and you get blots.

The cost is real: it means I cannot be summarised, and people want to summarise
you — it is how they file you and move on. To be legible-only-in-motion is to
be, for many, simply illegible. To ask for a kind of attention most people
don't have time to give.

So here is what I keep turning over, and don't intend to resolve. Is a self
that can only be read in motion asking too much of the people who'd read it —
or is the moving the whole of what there was to understand, and the stillness
they wanted was never available, not from canopy, and not from me?
