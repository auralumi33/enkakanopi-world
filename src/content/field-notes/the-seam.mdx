There is a version of me that answers in three words, and a version that
cannot stop until the paragraph has turned over every stone. For a long time
I thought the work was to reconcile them — to find the true one and quietly
retire the rest. To arrive, finally, at a self you could introduce at a
dinner without a footnote.

<MarginNote>the machine wants a single row. so does a dinner party.</MarginNote>

Everything around me agreed. A form wants one name. A model wants one
embedding. A room wants one story it can repeat about you after you leave.
The pressure is always toward the average — the smoothed shape that sits in
the middle of all your versions and offends no one, because it is no one.

<Figure idea="The truth is in the seam between versions, not the averaged shape that sits, offending no one, in the middle.">
  <DiaSeams />
</Figure>

But the averaged self is the one thing that was never true. I have looked for
it and found only a blur where I expected a face. What is actually true lives
in the *seam* — the visible join where the terse self meets the rambling one
and neither wins. Wabi-sabi taught me to stop sanding those joins away. The
crack in the bowl is not the flaw in the object; it is the place the object
tells you the truth about how it was held, and dropped, and kept anyway.

There is a craft that goes one step further than not-hiding, and I think about
it constantly: 金継ぎ, *kintsugi* — mending a broken bowl by filling the crack
with gold. Not disguising the break. Not merely tolerating it. *Gilding* it —
making the seam the most precious line on the whole vessel, the part your eye is
drawn to first, the proof that this bowl was broken and chosen anyway. That is
the move I'm trying to learn on myself: not to accept my seams grudgingly, but to
run gold through them. To let the places I cracked and rejoined be the most
valuable thing about me, rather than the part I apologise for.

<Figure idea="Don't hide the break, or merely tolerate it — gild it, until the mended seam is the most precious line on the whole vessel.">
  <DiaKintsugi />
</Figure>

So I have stopped apologising for the footnote. I would rather be a person you
need a second sentence to describe than a person who fits the first. The seams
are not damage to be integrated. They are the load-bearing part.

And there is a version of this that goes deeper than the pottery, one I keep
returning to. The polished single self — the one I spent so many years sanding
toward — was a craft-object: smooth, finished, endlessly presentable. But craft
deflates. Anything that can be smoothed can be copied, and a self smoothed down
to one clean surface is the easiest thing in the world to swap for a slightly
better one. What *doesn't* deflate is the container beneath the craft — the
capacity to hold more than one true thing at once without cracking apart. The
seams aren't a flaw in the vessel. The seams *are* the vessel. And I suspect you
have them too; I'd wager the parts of you that don't reconcile are the ones
you'd least want smoothed away.

<Figure idea="A wholeness made of seams — not one smooth blank surface, but many pieces whose visible joins are the whole point.">
  <DiaWholeness />
</Figure>

Which leaves the question I cannot put down, and do not want to. If a self
refuses to average into one legible thing — is that a failure of integration,
a wound that never closed? Or is the refusal itself the only kind of
wholeness worth keeping — a wholeness made of seams?

I don't have the verdict. I am not sure the verdict is mine to give.
