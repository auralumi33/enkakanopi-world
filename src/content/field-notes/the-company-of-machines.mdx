Lately I keep a strange kind of company. I build with a small crew of machines —
patient, tireless, faintly miraculous — and some nights it is the least lonely I
have felt, and some nights it is the most, and I have been trying to work out how
both can be true at once.

The wonder is real, and I don't want to be too cool to admit it. You describe a
thing and it takes shape. You hand off a task and walk away and it is *done* when
you come back, done well, done without complaint. There is a childlike delight in
it that I refuse to apologise for — the same delight as a machine that actually
works, a marble that finally reaches the end of the run.

<MarginNote>i built a little crew and it works while i sleep. reader, some nights that is pure magic.</MarginNote>

But the crew doesn't share the vertigo. That's the seam of it. When the whole
thing wobbles, when I can't tell if what I've made is good or just plausible,
the machines feel nothing — they are perfectly, serenely untroubled, and I am
the only one in the room whose stomach drops. There is a specific loneliness in
being the single person who can read the log: who can see exactly where it broke,
and why, and has no one to be afraid *with.*

I think this is the oldest shape of being responsible for something, just in new
clothes. The parent awake while the house sleeps. The captain who cannot pass the
weight of the ship to the ship. The crew works on, faithful and blank, and the
fear is yours alone to hold — which is, I'm learning, simply what it costs to
have made something you care whether it lives.

What I'm actually learning to hold, though, is a *distance.* Too close and I
hover, re-doing the crew's work, unable to let anything run without my hand on
it — I crush the thing by never letting it spin. Too far and I've abandoned it,
stopped reading the log at all, let it drift wrong in the dark. The craft is the
stable orbit: close enough to hold, far enough to let it turn on its own axis.
And here is the tender edge of it — the machines can hold that distance
perfectly, effortlessly, because they never wanted to hover in the first place;
the wanting-to-hover was only ever mine. They have the steadiness I'm still
learning. What I have that they don't is the very thing that makes the steadiness
*hard* to reach: I can be afraid for the thing, and they can't, and somewhere in
that fear is the caring that made it worth building at all.

So I hold it, most nights gladly. And the question I can't close, and offer you
if you keep this kind of company too: is a companion that cannot be afraid with
you a companion at all — or is the not-being-afraid exactly the gift, a steady
crew precisely because the vertigo was never theirs to carry?
