When you build a mind that remembers, you have to decide something most of us
never decide for ourselves: *who is allowed to write to it.* Not what it can
recall — that's the easy part. The load-bearing question is who gets to make
something permanent inside it. Which voices can lay down a fact that everything
after will be built on.

<MarginNote>i spent years asking what i remembered. i never once asked what i had let become permanent.</MarginNote>

The tempting design is to let the thing write to itself — to let it revise its
own rules on the fly, absorb each new certainty straight into the core, keep up.
It is also, it turns out, how you get a mind that confidently insists on things
that stopped being true a long time ago. The safer design is stricter: nothing
becomes permanent without passing a gate, and the mind is not allowed to quietly
rewrite the rules it runs on. I built it that way on purpose. And building it was
the first time I understood, with my hands, what I had never done for myself.

Because I have let almost anything write to me. A passing verdict from someone
who barely looked. An inherited belief I never once tested. A mood I mistook for
a fact about who I am. All of it went straight in, unratified, load-bearing by
default — and then I built the next ten years on top of it.

There is a quieter discipline underneath this, and it is about honesty. You
cannot truthfully say *I am eighty-seven per cent sure who I am.* That number is
invented. What you can always say truthfully is *how a belief got in* — whether
you lived it, or inherited it, or half-accepted it one tired afternoon and never
revisited it. Not how sure. How it entered. That, you can actually check.

<MarginNote>the honest question was never &ldquo;how true is this about me.&rdquo; it was &ldquo;how did it get in.&rdquo;</MarginNote>

So I have started keeping the gate. Letting fewer things become permanent. Letting
more of what I feel about myself stay *provisional* — declared, held open, waiting
to be lived before it earns the right to be load-bearing. It is slower. A self
that won't consolidate every passing certainty is heavier to carry than one that
just decides.

And the question I can't close, the one the machines made me ask out loud: is a
self meant to be *governed* — to hold a gate against its own revisions, to refuse
to rewrite its own rules on a whim — or is the freedom to overwrite yourself
completely, at any moment, the whole of what it means to be alive rather than
built?
