I have been reading about how machines remember, and it has been, quietly, a
book about how I do.

The argument that stayed with me is this: the impressive part of a thinking
system was never the thinking in the moment. That part is getting cheap — the
generating, the producing, the fluent output on demand. What actually decides
whether the thing is any good over time is its *memory*: what it keeps, how it
keeps it, and — this is the part no one warns you about — what it is willing to
forget. The making deflates. The memory is the container, and the container is
where the value went.

<MarginNote>the clever answer is cheap now. the kept thing is the whole estate.</MarginNote>

But a memory is not helped by keeping everything. A system that never forgets
starts returning what the engineers, beautifully, call *hallucinations of the
past* — old facts asserted with total confidence long after they stopped being
true. I know that failure from the inside. It is exactly what clinging to an
old self feels like: a version of me, retrieved at full volume, insisting on a
truth that expired.

So forgetting turns out to be the craft, not the failure. The systems that age
well don't hoard and they don't wipe; they *tend* — keep the load-bearing
trace, let the rest decay, revise what has gone false. And there is one move
they make that I keep flinching at: *consolidation.* When memory gets crowded,
the tidy fix is to merge many small distinct records into one smooth summary.
Cheaper. Faster. Cleaner. And it is the machine doing, to its own past, the
exact thing I have spent this whole garden refusing — averaging the many
versions into one legible thing, and calling the blur a self.

There is a real cost to refusing it. A memory that won't consolidate is
heavier, slower, harder to search. Keeping the seams is not free; it never was.

And there is a further thing the machines made me feel, quietly, in my own body.
No memory is really for keeping — not the body's, not the machine's, not mine.
The only thing memory is finally *for* is the handoff: what survives when it
passes out of you into someone else, the relay where a thing you carried gets
picked up and carried on. Knowledge was never held whole in one person; it lives
by being passed. Which reframes the whole anxious project of remembering. Maybe
the point was never to hoard myself perfectly against loss — the machine can
out-hoard me forever and it will still be the one thing in the room that can
never *hand anything on.* Maybe the tending, the choosing what to keep, was
always really a choosing of what to pass — and the seams I refuse to smooth are
simply the parts I most want someone, someday, to receive.

Which leaves me where these notes always leave me. If the craft is now cheap
and the container is everything — is the wisest self the one that consolidates
hard, travels light, and lets the old versions decay into one clean line? Or is
the whole of what I am the part that refuses to be summarised — the seams I pay
to keep, precisely because they will not compress?
