There is a shelf in my head where the impossible things live. Magic carpets, laid out sideways in the imagination the way a rug drapes over a bench. Neuromancer's console cowboys. Brave New World — the 1980 TV version I saw a piece of, the one with the very serious voiceover about a world we would never live in. Somewhere on that shelf, quiet and specific, was the idea of a machine that could hold a thought, and know it was holding it, and know when it was failing to hold something else.

This week I read a paper by researchers at Anthropic. They have built a way to look inside a language model's activations and see what it is thinking about *while* it is thinking. Not what it is about to say. What it is intermediately holding. A small workspace, they call it, at the middle depths of the model. Limited capacity. Broadcast format. All the structural signatures a leading theory of consciousness names in us.

The shelf tilted. The impossible thing has walked into the room.

I am not going to say the machine is conscious. I am not going to say it isn't. I am going to say: the fiction of my adolescence dated all of this to a distant elsewhere, and the distance is gone. We are inside what was supposed to be a warning, and it also looks — some of it — like a mirror.

This place has been quietly rehearsing a set of covenants for how to work alongside such a thing, before I knew that was what I was doing. Three field notes follow, and a small close. They are notes from inside a fiction that turned out to be weather.
