I built something small that could work without me. The strange part was not
building it. The strange part was walking out of the room while it kept going.

For most of my life, care and oversight were the same word. If I loved a
thing, I watched it. I stayed. I checked. Control felt like devotion, and I
never once caught the substitution happening — that I had quietly swapped
*trusting the thing* for *never taking my eyes off it*.

<MarginNote>i kept calling it care. it was surveillance wearing care&rsquo;s coat.</MarginNote>

Then I made a thing whose whole point was to run when I wasn't looking. And I
stood at the door with my hand still half-raised toward it, the way you reach
after something already falling, and I had to decide whether the reaching was
help or just the shape my fear makes with a body.

Letting go turned out to be the hardest craft I have tried to learn — harder
than the making. There is a specific grief in it that no one warns you about:
not the grief of losing the thing, but the grief of it being *fine* without
you. Of standing outside a room where the work is happening correctly, in your
absence, and feeling the exact size of your own smallness. Freed and bereft in
the same breath.

Here is what the room taught me, and I think it's coming for all of us: the
*making* is the part that's getting cheap. The execution — the doing of the
skill, the hours at the wheel — deflates a little more every year, as the tools
get better at it than I am. What stays scarce, what actually appreciates, is the
thing I found hardest: the judgment about which container to build, and then the
trust to walk out of the room and let it hold. The hovering was me clinging to
the deflating part — the *doing* — because it was the part I knew how to be proud
of. Trust was being asked to value the harder, quieter thing instead.

I am told this is what trust costs, and that the cost is the proof. That a
person who can build the machine *and* leave the room is worth more than one
who can only hover. I believe it on the good days.

But I notice I still linger by the door. So I am left holding the thing I
can't yet resolve: is the lingering a failure of trust I should train out of
myself — or is it the honest human residue of having made something you love,
the part that would be colder to lose than to keep?
