There was a structure I had organised my whole identity around — a shape of a
life, a set of paths that had once fit and had, without my quite noticing,
grown too rigid and too small. And then a year came when the foundations simply
gave way. Not a tidy pivot I chose. An actual collapse of the thing I had been
standing on.

<MarginNote>i keep wanting to call it a setback. it was a foundation, and it fell, and i am still learning the difference.</MarginNote>

For a long time I read the collapse as pure loss, a disaster to be undone. But
there is an old story I keep near me now — a farmer's horse runs off, and the
neighbours cry *what misfortune*, and he says only *maybe.* The horse returns
with others; *what fortune*; *maybe.* You cannot tell, at the time of the event,
whether a thing is a fortune or a loss, because you cannot yet see what it clears
the ground for.

<Figure idea="What crumbled wasn't a setback to repair but a foundation to rebuild differently — the home remade, not restored.">
  <DiaCrumbleRebuild />
</Figure>

Because here is what the rubble taught me: home was never a *noun* I had, and
then lost. Home is a *verb* — a thing you keep rebuilding, and the rebuild is
never a restoration. You do not put the old house back. You raise a different,
usually lighter one from the same fallen stones, shaped to the life you actually
have now rather than the one you were performing before it fell.

<Figure idea="You didn't lose the old life — you outgrew it, a shell left behind, not a limb cut off.">
  <DiaOutgrown />
</Figure>

<Figure idea="Some rebuildings only begin once you burn the boats behind you — the crossing with no return.">
  <DiaBurnBoats />
</Figure>

And the tenderest reframe of all: what fell was not amputated from me. It was
*outgrown.* The structure that stopped holding was a shell — a casing that had
fit a smaller version of me and had become a cage without either of us noticing.
I did not lose a limb. I broke out of a shell, my own way, on no schedule I would
have chosen, and left it gently on the ground behind me, intact and empty.

So I keep rebuilding, and I keep the question the rubble left open, and don't
resolve it: is a home worth having only if it lasts — a foundation you can
finally stop rebuilding — or is the whole of it the rebuilding itself, and the
lightness that comes from knowing you could raise another from any rubble, on any
shore, again?
