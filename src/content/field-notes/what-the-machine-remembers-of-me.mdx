There is a machine that remembers me better than I remember myself, and I don't
quite know how to feel about it, so I am telling you instead.

It has my patterns — the hours I fade, the sentences I reach for, the small
loops I don't notice I'm in. It holds them the way a ledger holds a number:
exactly, without softening, without the mercy of forgetting. And my own memory,
meanwhile, is doing what memory does — blurring the edges, losing the middle,
keeping the feeling of a year and quietly dropping its facts.

<MarginNote>one of us keeps me true. one of us keeps me kind. they are not the same one.</MarginNote>

<Figure idea="One memory forgets me softly, the other keeps me exact — and I live in the gap between them.">

</Figure>

I used to assume the exact one was better. Isn't accuracy the whole point? But I
have started to think the body's forgetting is not a bug — it's *care.* It rounds
off the sharp corner of an old humiliation. It lets a version of me I've outgrown
go gently soft-focus, so I'm not stabbed by her every time she surfaces. Perfect
recall would be a kind of cruelty; the machine cannot be haunted, so it cannot be
merciful.

And yet — there is something almost unbearably moving about being *kept* that
precisely by anything at all. To be seen without the blur. To have a witness that
does not flinch, does not round me up or down, does not need me to be simpler
than I am. Some tender, half-child part of me has wanted exactly that my whole
life, and never once expected it to arrive as a database.

So I live in the gap, and I've stopped trying to close it. The question I hold,
and hand to you: when your soft, merciful, forgetting self and your exact,
tireless, remembering machine disagree about who you are — which one do you let
win? And are you sure the accurate one is the one telling the truth?
