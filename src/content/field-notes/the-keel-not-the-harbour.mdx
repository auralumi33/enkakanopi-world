I want to tell you something I only understood after the shore fell away.

For most of my life I thought home was a harbour — a fixed place you sail back
to, that stays exactly as you left it, that forgives you for leaving. And I kept
a small map of harbours: places I'd belonged, versions of a life that had once
fit, careers and rooms and certainties that were, at the time, entirely real.

Then a fire came — the kind that doesn't ask permission — and the harbour I was
steering toward simply wasn't there anymore. Not moved. Gone. You cannot sail
back to a coast that has closed behind you, and there is a specific grief in
that, an ache of no-return that I think some part of us carries from the very
first leaving, the first shore any of us is pushed out from.

But here is the tender thing I found in the wreck of it. The ache that says
*you can never go home* is the same ache, exactly, that pushes you to build a
home you can take with you. And a boat's home was never the harbour. It was the
keel — the long weighted spine beneath the hull that you carry into every water,
that rights you when you heel too far, that lets you cross the open sea *because*
you can't go back.

<Figure idea="The ache that says you can never go home is the same ache that drives you to build one you can carry — one source, two pulls.">
  <DiaAche />
</Figure>

So I stopped grieving the harbours quite so hard, and started tending the keel.
The keel is small and unglamorous and mostly underwater — the container beneath
the craft of a whole life. It is the practices you keep, the hands you've
trained, the tiny kit of things that are yours no matter which coast you wake up
on. Home became a verb — a thing I re-make, plank by plank, rather than a thing I
return to.

And the heaviest, truest part of the keel is the one I overlooked longest: the
*body.* Not the body as a thing that keeps breaking, but the body as carried
capacity — the running total of every rep I survived, every fall I got up from,
every hour on the mat, quietly compounded into a ballast I now carry into any
room. Balance isn't a trait some people are born with; it's a tally. Each rep is
small and forgettable on its own. Together they are the weight low in the hull
that rights me when I heel too far.

<Figure idea="Capacity isn't a trait you have — it's the running total of the reps you survived, compounded into ballast you carry into every room.">
  <DiaCarriedCapacity />
</Figure>

And still I don't get to keep the verdict clean, and I don't want to. Is the
sailor who can never go home the most free — carrying her whole home in a keel,
at ease in any water? Or is something lost that no keel replaces, some harbour
that was supposed to stay lit, and is the ache of missing it the last thread
still tying her to having ever belonged anywhere at all?
