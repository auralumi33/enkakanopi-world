For years I treated the scatter of me as a problem of *arithmetic.* If there
were many of me — the one who works in one language and the one who only surfaces
in another, the maker and the one who lets go, the self that runs ahead and the
self that stays behind to grieve — then surely the task was to add them up,
average them out, and arrive at a single sum I could sign.

<MarginNote>i kept trying to solve for one. the pieces were never an equation.</MarginNote>

But you cannot average a poem. And that, it turns out, is what the pieces are —
not terms in a sum but stanzas of one poem, written across a life that would
never fit onto a single line. They do not reconcile. They are not *supposed* to.
What they do — and it took me an embarrassingly long time to hear it — is
*rhyme.* An echo leaps the gap between one fragment and another; a rhythm from
the terse self answers, faintly, in the rambling one; the maker and the one who
lets go turn out to be the same rhyme, sounded twice.

</Figure>

And here is the shape underneath the shape, the thing I am only beginning to
trust. The many do not collapse into one — but they *do* gather, into pairs. Held
in tension: to run ahead and to stay and grieve; to hold a thing close and to let
it spin; the mother tongue and the acquired one, each saying what the other
can't. Multiplicity, it turns out, is most livable held as *duality* — not one
blurred average, and not an infinite chaos of selves, but a set of poised
opposites that balance precisely because neither wins.

So I have stopped doing the arithmetic. I read myself now the way you read a
poem, listening for the rhymes and letting the tensions stand.

And the question I leave open, the way this place always does: is a self held as
poised opposites a kind of peace I've finally made — or is the balancing itself
the endless work, a duet that only sounds like harmony because I never let either
voice finish its sentence?
