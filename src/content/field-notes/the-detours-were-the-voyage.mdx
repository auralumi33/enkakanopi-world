I keep wanting my reinvention to have taken the straight line. Cast off, cross,
arrive — clean, quick, a tidy before-and-after I could tell at a dinner without
a footnote. And I keep being handed, instead, the long way round: the detours,
the setbacks, the small islands I didn't plan to land on and couldn't leave when
I wanted to.

For a long time I read all of it as the *delay* — the annoying middle before the
real story started. The straight line was the voyage; the wandering was just my
failure to sail it properly.

<MarginNote>i kept apologising for the scenic route. as if the route were a detour from my life, and not the whole of it.</MarginNote>

<Figure idea="The straight line home was never on offer — the detours were the voyage.">
  <DiaQuest />
</Figure>

But the oldest voyages we still tell were never straight. The hero doesn't get
the direct crossing; she gets the storms and the strange islands and the years
that weren't in the plan, and the *point* of the telling is precisely those —
not the arrival. Nobody remembers the calm passages. The detours weren't
obstacles between her and the story. They *were* the story, and each trial was a
landfall where she picked up something she'd have no other way to carry.

<Figure idea="Nobody remembers the calm passages — it's the storms, the trials, that get gilded into the story worth telling.">
  <DiaStorms />
</Figure>

This is a kinder way to hold a life remade slowly and against your wishes: form
follows life, not the plan you drew for it. The straight line was never on offer
— not to me, not to anyone who's actually crossed anything — and the spirit of
adventure isn't the calm of a clean passage. It's the willingness to make a
landfall of whatever island you wash up on, and take from it the one thing that
was only available there.

<Figure idea="Each trial is a landfall where you pick up the one thing only that island held — something you'd have no other way to carry.">
  <DiaLandfall />
</Figure>

So I'm trying to stop apologising for the scenic route. And the question I sail
on, and leave open for you: is the long way round a thing you endure on the way
to who you'll become — or is the arriving beside the point entirely, and the
whole of the life just the quality of attention you brought to the detours?
