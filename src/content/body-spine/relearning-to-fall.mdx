For most of a year my body was not a place I could fully live in. Something had
gone wrong in the deep architecture of it, the kind of wrong that takes months
and not weeks to come back from, and the coming back was slow enough that I got
to watch it happen one small permission at a time.

When I could finally move again in earnest, I put on skates. This was, by any
sensible measure, backwards. You do not hand a newly-mended body the one
activity whose entire premise is falling.

<MarginNote>everyone said start gentle. i wanted to negotiate with the floor directly.</MarginNote>

But that was the instinct, and I have learned to trust the instincts that
arrive without an argument attached. Because here is what skating actually
teaches, before it teaches anything about grace: it teaches you how to fall.
Not how to avoid falling — that is a beginner's misunderstanding — but how to
*meet the ground* without bracing, how to turn a drop into a roll, how to let
the fall be information instead of catastrophe.

</Figure>

My whole life my head had overruled my body on exactly this point. The head's
strategy for falling is to stiffen — to refuse the fall, to fight the floor,
which is precisely how you get hurt. The body, left to its own counsel, knows
better. It knows to go soft, to spread the cost across a curve, to give the
impact somewhere to travel. The head calls that giving-up. The body calls it
landing.

Relearning to fall was, I think, the real beginning of relearning to trust the
body as an instrument at all — to grant it authority in the specific rooms
where it is simply more competent than I am. The head had been driving for
years, badly, on roads it couldn't feel.

So the question I keep, sitting on the ice with a fresh bruise and no regret:
have I finally learned to let the body lead where it knows better — or is
handing over the wheel just another thing the head decided to do, still
managing, still in charge of its own surrender?
