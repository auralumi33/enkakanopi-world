The word the movement work kept giving me was *articulation.* Roll down through
the spine one vertebra at a time. Feel where the sentence runs on, where it
skips, where three segments have fused into a single stiff clause that moves
only as a block.

I had thought of my back as one thing — a rod, a mast, a single object that was
either upright or not. That is how it felt: all or nothing, held or collapsed.
But a spine is not a rod. It is closer to a sentence: a long line of small
parts, and the meaning lives in the punctuation between them — the pauses, the
joins, the places it is allowed to bend.

To articulate the spine is to go back through that sentence and restore the
punctuation the years had sanded flat. Here a comma that had hardened into a
full stop. Here a run-on where two vertebrae had forgotten they were separate
words. The work is not to build a stronger rod. It is to teach a fused thing to
move as parts again — to put the small silences back between the bones.

<MarginNote>i wasn&rsquo;t stiff. i was over-punctuated in the wrong places — full stops where there should have been breath.</MarginNote>

There is something almost unbearably apt in this, for a person who has spent
her life with sentences. The body was keeping a record in exactly the grammar
I understood, and I had been too far up in my head to read it. The tension I
carried literally, structurally, was a punctuation problem: everything held,
nothing allowed to pause and continue. A spine that could only stand or fall,
never *phrase.*

So I re-punctuate, slowly, one segment at a time, and I feel the old sentence
become sayable in new ways. And I am left with the question the body poses and
does not answer: is the goal to make the spine finally *fluent* — smooth, fully
articulated, resolved — or is a body, like a self, meant to keep some of its
stiff clauses and awkward breaks, because the places it refuses to move are
also part of what it has to say?
