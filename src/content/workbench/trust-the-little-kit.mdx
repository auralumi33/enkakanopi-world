When I was small I could make a whole afternoon out of almost nothing — a few
blocks, a length of string, a box that was a boat and then a house and then a
boat again. I didn't need the fancy set. I needed a handful of pieces I trusted
and a grammar for snapping them together, and the rest was just play.

Somewhere along the way I lost faith in the little kit. I started believing I
needed the big box — the complete toolkit, the right materials, the credentials,
the whole expensive set before I was allowed to make anything real. And the big
box, it turns out, is mostly a way of never starting. Especially now: as the
deflation of execution hands the polished object to any machine that asks, the
one scarce thing left is the nerve to begin — badly, small, from a kit you trust.

So on the workbench I am practising the opposite. A tiny kit: a line, a curve, a
box, a leaf, a fold, a dot. Six little primitives and a few honest rules for how
they join. Everything I'm learning to make — a drawing, a small machine, a page,
a whole visual language — comes out of that handful, recombined. Not because I
can't afford more. Because the constraint is the generator: a small kit you
*trust* invents more than a big one you're afraid of.

And underneath the primitives, the realer thing I'm relearning is to trust my
*hands.* To let them start before I've fully thought it through, to believe the
fingers know something the head hasn't caught up to yet. The head wants the whole
plan first. The hands just want to begin, and be forgiven for the wobble, and try
the next one. Every ruined little object is the hands learning out loud.

Which leaves me the question I keep turning on the bench, and pass to you if your
hands are also out of practice: is the small trusted kit a beginner's constraint
I'll outgrow once I'm "properly" equipped — or is trusting a handful of pieces and
your own two hands the entire craft, the thing the big box was always only
pretending to replace?
